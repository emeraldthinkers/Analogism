# Analogism

**An open, evolving contemporary art movement exploring the relationship between technology, art, and the human experience.**

Analogism is not defined by a single aesthetic, medium, generation, or ideology. It is a shared cultural framework for artists, designers, writers, musicians, filmmakers, technologists, researchers, craftspeople, curators, educators, and makers examining how emerging technologies shape perception, creativity, identity, memory, intimacy, labor, culture, and society.

The movement is intentionally decentralized. It has no prescribed style, fixed doctrine, official membership body, or exclusive authority. It grows through participation, interpretation, criticism, translation, and contribution.

Some participants embrace artificial intelligence, robotics, virtual environments, computational media, and emerging technologies as creative partners. Others turn toward slow, handmade, embodied, or entirely analog processes. Many occupy the territory between these positions.

Analogism does not demand one answer.

**The dialogue is the movement.**

## Participate

You may participate by creating work, writing criticism, organizing exhibitions, translating the manifesto, developing tools, proposing revisions, opening discussions, teaching the framework, or forming an independent interpretation.

Participation does not require permission. It does require a meaningful engagement with the relationship between technology, art, and human experience.

## Attribution

The manifesto is collectively and intentionally unattributed. Contributors may be recognized through commit history, pull requests, acknowledgments, and translation notes, while the public text remains focused on the movement rather than on founder identity.
