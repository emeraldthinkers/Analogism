# The Analogism Manifesto

**Public Draft v1.0.0**

Analogism is an open, evolving contemporary art movement dedicated to exploring the relationship between technology, art, and the human experience.

It is not defined by a single aesthetic, medium, generation, or ideology. It is defined by a shared commitment to examining how technology reshapes perception, creativity, identity, memory, intimacy, labor, culture, and society.

Analogism begins from a simple condition: technology is no longer merely a tool outside of human life. It has become an environment within it.

We live among systems that predict, recommend, record, simulate, automate, translate, generate, rank, and remember. These systems increasingly influence what we see, what we believe, what we make, and how we understand ourselves. Art cannot remain separate from this condition, because art is one of the places where a culture confronts what it is becoming.

Analogism exists for that confrontation.

## I. No Prescribed Style

Analogism has no required visual language.

It may appear as oil painting, sculpture, photography, film, writing, music, performance, installation, code, artificial intelligence, virtual space, physical craft, or forms not yet named.

A work is not Analogist because of how it looks. It becomes part of Analogism through the questions it carries.

Those questions may include:

- What remains human when creation is increasingly shared with machines?
- What happens to memory when everything can be stored, altered, and reproduced?
- What happens to identity when the self becomes an image, profile, dataset, or simulation?
- What happens to culture when attention is measured and traded?
- What is gained through technological progress, and what is quietly abandoned?
- Can slowness, touch, imperfection, and physical presence become radical acts?
- Can technology reveal dimensions of experience that traditional methods cannot?

Analogism does not demand one answer.

It preserves the necessity of asking.

## II. No Authorized Position

Within Analogism, no single position on technology is authoritative.

Some artists embrace artificial intelligence, robotics, computational media, biotechnology, virtual environments, and emerging systems as collaborators capable of expanding artistic possibility.

Others turn toward painting, carving, weaving, handwriting, darkrooms, mechanical processes, live performance, and other embodied practices as a way of preserving forms of attention and knowledge that technological culture may neglect.

Many move between these positions.

Analogism does not divide the technological from the human into simple categories of progress and resistance. It recognizes that both enthusiasm and refusal can become forms of blindness.

The movement is located in the tension between them.

## III. The Analog Is Not the Opposite of Technology

In Analogism, the word *analog* does not mean nostalgia, purity, or escape.

It refers to continuity, relation, embodiment, and the presence of difference within resemblance. It names the imperfect transfer between experience and representation, between person and machine, between memory and record, between the physical world and its simulation.

The analog is not simply what existed before the digital.

It is what reminds us that no system fully contains the life it describes.

## IV. Participation Over Permission

Analogism is intentionally decentralized.

There is no governing academy, prescribed membership, official aesthetic, or final doctrine. No institution owns the questions from which the movement emerges.

Artists, designers, writers, musicians, filmmakers, researchers, technologists, craftspeople, and makers may participate by creating work, writing criticism, organizing exhibitions, translating texts, proposing revisions, developing tools, challenging assumptions, or forming independent interpretations.

Participation does not require permission.

The movement grows through use rather than obedience.

## V. Openness Without Meaninglessness

To be open does not mean that Analogism means everything.

Analogism remains centered on the changing relationship between technology, art, and human experience. Work associated with the movement should engage that relationship with intention rather than using the name as decoration.

No participant is required to agree with the manifesto in full. Disagreement is not a threat to the movement; it is one of its methods.

But participation asks for seriousness, intellectual honesty, and good faith.

## VI. The Artist Is Not Obsolete

Analogism rejects the idea that technological capability makes human creativity irrelevant.

The role of the artist has never depended solely on technical difficulty. Art is not valuable because a machine cannot perform its visible labor. It is valuable because a person, community, or consciousness has chosen what to notice, what to question, what to reveal, and what to place into the world.

New tools may alter authorship, labor, skill, originality, and ownership. These changes must be examined openly.

But the artist remains necessary wherever meaning is not automatic.

## VII. The Machine Is Not Neutral

Analogism also rejects the fantasy that technology exists outside culture.

Every system carries assumptions. Every interface organizes behavior. Every dataset reflects decisions about what is included, excluded, valued, and ignored.

Artists working with technology are not merely using neutral instruments. They are entering systems shaped by economics, politics, labor, infrastructure, and power.

To engage technology seriously is to examine those conditions as part of the medium itself.

## VIII. Preservation and Transformation

Analogism values both preservation and transformation.

It recognizes the importance of safeguarding physical skills, local knowledge, material traditions, cultural memory, privacy, authorship, and direct human encounter.

It also recognizes that new technologies may create unfamiliar forms of perception, collaboration, access, and expression.

The movement does not ask culture to choose permanently between the old and the new.

It asks what deserves to survive, what deserves to change, and who is allowed to decide.

## IX. A Living Framework

This manifesto is not a finished doctrine.

It is a public framework designed to be read, questioned, translated, challenged, and developed. Its version history is preserved so that its evolution remains visible.

No revision can permanently settle the questions that gave rise to Analogism. The movement must remain responsive to technologies and cultural conditions that do not yet exist.

Its continuity depends not on fixed language, but on sustained inquiry.

## X. Declaration

We believe art must remain a place where humanity can examine the systems it creates before those systems become invisible.

We believe technological progress should be neither worshiped without criticism nor rejected without understanding.

We believe physical making, computational creation, historical memory, speculative imagination, and human presence can coexist without collapsing into a single doctrine.

We believe the future of art will not be determined by choosing between the human hand and the machine.

It will be determined by how consciously we define the relationship between them.

Analogism belongs to everyone who enters that relationship with intention.

**The dialogue is the movement.**
